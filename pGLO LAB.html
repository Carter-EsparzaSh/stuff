What is pGLO Transformation?

The pGLO transformation is an experiment where we introduce the pGLO plasmid into E. coli bacteria. The transformed bacteria gain the ability to produce Green Fluorescent Protein (GFP), making them glow bright green only under UV light

How pGLO Controls Gene Expression

The control part of pGLO is its arabinose operon system :

‚Ä¢ Without arabinose: araC protein blocks GFP transcription ‚Üí No glow

‚Ä¢ With arabinose: araC changes shape and activates GFP ‚Üí Bacteria glow green under UV

This gives us complete control over when the bacteria produce GFP by manipulating the presence of arabinose.

Arabinose Operon

When arabinose is absent, the AraC protein binds to the DNA and prevents RNA polymerase from binding. This prevents transcription of the digestive enzymes.
When arabinose is present, it interacts with AraC, causing AraC to change shape and allowing RNA polymerase to bind the promoter. This results in expression of araB, araA, and araD and subsequent breakdown of arabinose until the arabinose runs out.​
The pGLO plasmid contains both the promoter (pBAD) and araC gene, but araB, araA, and araD have been replaced by the single gene that codes for GFP.

How to Calculate Transformation Efficiency

To calculate the total amount of plasmid DNA that you plated, you need to know the original concentration of your DNA plasmid and the volume of plasmid that you add to the transformation mix. You also have to take into account that you add LB medium to the transformation mix to recover the cells and only use a fraction of that volume to actually spread on the agar plates.

The transformation efficiency is expressed as the number of colony-forming units (cfu) per microgram of plasmid DNA.
